Art. 31 & Art. 134

Summary

Article 31

  • You Cannot be Compelled to Incriminate Yourself
  • Similar to 5th Amendment & Miranda Rights
  • Article 31(b): “No person ... may interrogate, or request any statement from, an accused or a person suspected of an offense without first informing him of the nature of the accusation...“

Summary

Article 134

  • General Article - "Catch-All"
  • Clause 1: "neglect to the prejudice of good order and discipline in the armed forces"
  • Clause 2: "conduct of a nature to bring discredit upon the armed forces"
  • Clause 3: Non-Capital Offenses Violating other Federal Laws

UCMJ History

  • 1775 - Second Continental Congress established 69 Articles of War, governing Continental Army
  • 1788 - U.S. Constitution gave Congress power to regulate Land and Naval forces
  • 1806 - Congress revised to 101 Articles of War
  • Articles for the Government of the United States Navy - "Rocks and Shoals"
  • 1863 - Lieber Code, Civil War
  • 1920 - Congress revised to 121 Articles of War
  • 1951 - Uniform Code of Military Justice enacted

Overview

Article 31
  • Forbids forcing another to admit something incriminating
  • Protects from an uninformed interrogation; they must know the accusation
  • Forbids demanding unnecessary, degrading evidence
  • Any evidence procured in violation of the above, or any other law, is not admissable

Overview

Article 134
  • Grants power to prosecutors & juries, to charge criminals without needing a UCMJ article
  • Examples:
    • Animal Abuse
    • Bigamy - Marriage while already Married
    • Financial Fraud or Failure to Pay Debt
    • Disorderly Conduct / Drunkenness
    • Fraternization
    • Negligent Discharge
    • Extramarital Sexual Contact

Why Article 31 is Important

  • Defend from False Accusations
  • Force a Proper Proceeding
  • Maintain Order - Avoid a Kangaroo Court

Why Article 134 is Important

  • To Charge people who are obviously committing immoral acts
  • Extend the UCMJ over time with new exceptions to the laws
  • Maintain Order - Avoid Loopholes in Literal Interpretation

Conclusion

  • Article 31 protects plausible innocence
  • Article 134 assumes plausible guilt

Still performed with order of the court; avoids chaos while determining guilt or innocence.
