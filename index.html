Summary

Parris Island was a very significant addition to the Marine Corps:

  • Drastically Strengthened the Fighting Force
  • As a Symbol of Initiation to new Marines
  • As a Monument to the USMC Traditions

MCRD Parris Island has a rich USMC history:

  • First Female Trained
  • First African American Trained
  • First African American Drill Instructor

History - Civil War

Parris Island was harbored to many military settlers since the discovery of the New World - originally called "Port Royal", its Sound gave convenient access to the Atlantic and deeper inland.

During the Civil War, the Union Navy converted it from a cotton plantation to a coaling station; fueling the Union's maritime force. Two years after the war, the Navy and Army vacated most of the island, leaving a small unit to continue the supply.

History - Marines Arrive

In 1890, Navy officials decided to expand the supply and coaling depot to a repair facility - the expansion warranted Officer's housing, a Marine barracks, a Hospital, and a water closet (restroom).

The Marine guard consisted of 1 Sergeant, 2 Corporals, and 10 Privates.

History - Officers' School

By 1909, the Marine Barracks at Port Royal became Marine Officers' School, and shortly after, Disciplinary Barracks were created for the Navy; a Prison for criminal Sailors and Marines; guilty of embezzlement, unauthorized absence, and other similar crimes.

After maintaining good conduct in the prison for a few years, prisoners were given the option to re-enlist on probation.

History - Recruit Training

In 1911, Commandant Major General William P. Biddle ordered four recruit depots across the United States - Philadelphia, Norfolk, Puget Sound, and Mare Island. The Officers' School was also transferred to Norfolk, and continued to use Port Royal as a disciplinary installation.

Four years later, however, Norfolk was closed and moved back to Port Royal, for the extra space, dismantling the prison in the process. As the United States entered WWI, recruits surged from 835 on average, to a peak of 13,286.

Birth of the Depot

WWI demanded a large expansion from Port Royal, and it was during this time that MCO No. 32 ordered the redesignation of Marine Barracks, Port Royal to Marine Barracks, Parris Island.

The name "Parris Island" has an origin from before the Constitutional United States - it is named after a treasurer, Col. Alexander Parris, who long ago purchased the island in 1715.

History - WWI and WWII

More than 46,000 Marines were trained for duty for WWI. For WWII, over 200,000 Recruits became Marines, in over 13 recruit training battalions.

Shortly after the second world war, the 3th recruit training battalion was activated for training Female Marines (later redesignated as 4th RTBn), and likewise the African-Americans trained at Montford Point were integrated into the depot.

Implications

  • The large influx of recruits for WWI caused a new rank to be made - Private First Class.
  • The Drill Instructor billet was created at the same time, as was Drill Instructor School.
  • Standardized the requirements of being a Marine.

Conclusion

Parris Island was the first large-scale Recruit Depot for the Marine Corps; without it, we wouldn't have the same level of discipline, motivation, or leadership that we do today.
